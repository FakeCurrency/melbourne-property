    How to use Melbourne Property

    This tool helps you compare Melbourne suburbs across two main goals:

    • Live or rent — where is it safer and more stable for you or your family?
    • Invest or develop — where is there genuine potential for growth or redevelopment?

    Switch views using the mode buttons and presets. Everything updates instantly.

    Quick tips

    • Click any suburb on the map or in the list to see its full scorecard.
    • Use the slider to blend Liveability and Development Potential.
    • Try the one-click presets (Family First, Balanced Investor, Value-Add/Developer).
    • Hit Compare on a scorecard, then tap a second suburb for a side-by-side.
    • Hit Ask and type a budget + goal — “$500k to live”, “$800k to invest” — for instant matches.
    • Search accepts suburb names, postcodes (e.g. 3150) and street addresses.
    • Colour the map by Greenfield or Infill to separate the two development stories.
    • Customise weights rebuilds every score with your own priorities.
    • Toggle train stations on the map.
    • Copy the URL any time — it restores your exact view, suburb, comparison and custom weights.

    Colouring the map

    The map colour changes with what you select:

    • Live mode — emphasises safety, socio-economic advantage and family-friendly areas (green tones).
    • Balanced mode — a 50/50 mix of liveability and development potential.
    • Invest / Develop mode — highlights redevelopment headroom, price growth and infrastructure support (blue-gold tones).

    Key controls

    • Slider: how much weight you give Liveability vs Development.
    • Colour by: what the map colours represent (Overall, Safety, Growth, Yield…).
    • Hide weaker areas: focus only on stronger suburbs.

    What the scores mean (plain English)

    Liveability Score

    Higher = better for living or renting. Combines:

    • Lower rates of crimes against the person (now suburb-level)
    • Higher socio-economic advantage (SEIFA)
    • Closer train stations and schools (measured from residential land)
    • More stable, owner-occupied housing
    • Less flood / bushfire overlay coverage
    • Higher share of families with children (in Family mode)
    Development Potential Score

    Higher = more signals the area may have room to grow or be redeveloped. Combines:

    • Planning zoning — share of land zoned for intensification (RGZ, MUZ, ACZ, the new HCTZ activity-centre zone…) vs protective zoning (NRZ, green wedge), from Vicmap Planning
    • Higher proportion of detached houses (redevelopment headroom)
    • Recent price growth momentum and gross rental yield
    • Train-station proximity (activity-centre uplift) and electricity-grid support
    • Less Heritage Overlay coverage (heritage controls constrain rebuilds)
    • Less flood (LSIO/SBO/FO) and bushfire (BMO) overlay coverage

    Two sub-lenses split the story: Greenfield (UGZ corridors — estate-scale land supply) and Infill (upzoned, station-centred established suburbs). Colour the map by either from the chips.

    Overall Score & grades

    A blend of the two scores above — you control the balance with the slider or presets. Liveability and Development are rank-based (a 90 means better than ~90% of Greater Melbourne). The letter grade is a relative tier of the Overall score at the default 50/50 blend: A+ = top ~10%, A = next 15%, B = next 20%, C = next 25%, D = the rest.

    Suburbs with almost no residents (airports, industrial estates) are flagged "Employment precinct" — liveability signals aren't meaningful there. Where an input has no data for an area it is excluded and the remaining weights renormalised; the scorecard's "Data coverage" line tells you exactly what was used.

    Chip & term glossary

    Very safe / Safe — Low rates of personal crime
    Growth corridor — Strong recent price growth + redevelopment headroom
    Redevelopment headroom — High share of detached houses on larger blocks
    Affordable upside — Relatively lower prices with growth momentum
    Premium market — High prices, typically established or blue-chip areas
    Tightly held — High owner-occupier rates, lower rental turnover
    Family-friendly — Higher share of families with children + strong safety signals
    Zoned for growth — 20%+ of land already upzoned (RGZ/MUZ/ACZ/HCTZ)
    Flood / Bushfire overlay — 25%+ of land under LSIO/SBO/FO or BMO controls
    Heritage constrained — 25%+ of land under a Heritage Overlay
    Employment precinct — Almost no residents (airport/industrial); liveability signals not meaningful

    Where the data comes from

    Electricity Infrastructure

    Geoscience Australia — National Electricity Transmission Lines and Substations (2024). Proximity to transmission lines and density of substations within 10 km are used as practical signals for development feasibility. Detailed substation capacity data is generally not publicly available.

    AEMO context is referenced for the broader electricity network; geometry comes from Geoscience Australia.

    Property Prices & Growth

    Victorian Valuer-General / Land.Vic — Median house and unit prices by suburb (both 2014–2024 series). 12-month change, 3-year CAGR and the scorecard sparkline are calculated from this data.

    Liveability inputs
    • Crime Statistics Agency Victoria (CSA) — criminal incidents by suburb/town (LGA fallback where unmatched), split into crimes against the person vs property crime (year ending March 2026).
    • Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) — SEIFA 2021 (IRSAD & IEO), Census 2021 housing (G37) and age demographics (G01), plus Regional Population ERP at June 2025 for current populations, density and crime-rate denominators.
    • DTP / PTV — every metro + V/Line station (with annual patronage), FY2024-25.
    • Department of Education — School Locations 2025, all sectors.
    Rents & Yield

    DFFH Rental Report — moving annual median rents by suburb (Sep 2025 quarter), with LGA medians for growth suburbs the fixed DFFH list never named. Gross yield = 3-bed-house annual rent ÷ VG median house price.

    Planning & Zoning

    Vicmap Planning / VicPlan — planning-scheme zone polygons plus the Heritage, flood (LSIO, SBO, FO) and bushfire (BMO) overlays, sampled on a grid across each suburb. Zones are grouped into growth (RGZ, MUZ, ACZ, CCZ, C1Z, new HCTZ), standard (GRZ, UGZ precincts) and protective (NRZ, LDRZ, green wedge, farming). The residentially-zoned sample points also anchor the station/school distance measures, so big fringe suburbs are measured from where people live. Two development sub-lenses are computed: Greenfield (UGZ corridors, land supply) and Infill (upzoned, station-centred established suburbs).

    Geography

    ABS ASGS Edition 3 (2021) — Statistical Area Level 2 (SA2) boundaries for Greater Melbourne.

    Remaining limitations
    Electricity spare capacity is commercially sensitive, so grid support uses proximity/density proxies. Height limits and non-heritage overlays (DDO etc.) aren't scored yet. Each scorecard's "Data coverage" line tells you when a figure is LGA-level rather than suburb-level.
    Not financial advice
    This is a data exploration tool built from public statistics. Scores compare suburbs relative to each other — they are not valuations, forecasts or personal advice. Always do your own due diligence and speak to licensed professionals before buying.
