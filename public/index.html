    Guide & data sources

    Liveable Melbourne scores all 361 Greater Melbourne suburbs (ABS areas) from 0–100 on two things — how good they are to live in, and their potential to invest in or develop. Higher is always better; neither goal is “better” than the other — it depends what you’re looking for.

    The two scores, in plain English

    🏠
    LiveabilityHow good is it to live or rent here right now? Built from personal safety, socio-economic advantage and a settled housing mix.
    🏗️
    Development potential preliminary How much room is there to add value — build, subdivide or invest? Built from low-density housing, recent price growth and electricity-grid support.
    ⚖️
    OverallA blend of the two. The slider sets the mix; a preset picks it for you.

    Use it in 10 seconds

    1. Tap a “Show me the best” button, or a preset — the map recolours and the list re-ranks.
    2. Higher-scoring areas stand out in colour. Skim the ranked list in the panel.
    3. Click any suburb for its full scorecard and plain-English read-outs.

    Slider, modes & presets

    • Slider — drag Liveability ↔ Development to weight the Overall score.
    • Modes (Live / Balanced / Invest) — change the colour palette; Live mode also leans the liveability weighting toward families.
    • Presets — one tap sets the slider, palette and map colour for a goal.
    • Show me the best — highlights the top ~20 areas for living, investing or developing.

    What makes a score high or low?

    High Liveability lower personal-crime rate · higher socio-economic advantage (SEIFA) · more settled, owner-occupied housing · (Live mode) more children & schools nearby
    High Development more low-density detached housing (room to redevelop) · stronger recent price growth · better electricity-grid support · more rental turnover
    A low score is simply the opposite of these — it isn’t “bad”. A quiet, built-up blue-chip suburb scores high to live and low to develop; a new outer estate often does the reverse.

    Every input is ranked against all other Melbourne suburbs (a 0–100 percentile), so a score is relative to Melbourne, not an absolute rating.

          Where the data comes from

          🗺️
          Suburb boundaries ABS SA2 (2021)

          The ~suburb-sized areas every score is attached to (361 across Greater Melbourne).

          🛡️
          Crime Crime Statistics Agency Victoria

          Recorded offences (year ending Mar 2026), split into crimes against the person (assault, robbery, sexual offences) and property crime. Published per council (LGA), so suburbs in one council currently share a rate.

          📊
          Socio-economic ABS SEIFA 2021

          A relative measure of an area’s advantage/disadvantage (IRSAD) and education & occupation (IEO). Higher decile = more advantaged, relative to Australia.

          🏘️
          Housing & family ABS Census 2021

          Owner-occupier vs renter mix, detached houses vs apartments, and the share of children aged 0–14 — used for housing stability, redevelopment headroom and family signals.

          💰
          Property prices & growth Victorian Valuer-General · Land.Vic

          Median house & unit sale prices (10-year series). We show the latest median, 12-month change and 3-year growth. 3-year growth is the figure used in scoring (it smooths small-sample swings).

          ⚡
          Electricity infrastructure AEMO & Geoscience Australia

          National transmission lines and substations. We use distance to the grid and substation density as practical signals of how feasible a larger connection is. Note: detailed substation spare capacity is generally not public, so proximity and density are proxies — they indicate support, not a guaranteed connection.

          What’s preliminary / coming next

          • Development potential is preliminary — it shows feasibility signals, not a forecast, and not a guarantee you can build.
          • Not yet included: planning zoning & overlays (e.g. VICPLAN — what each lot is actually zoned for, plus heritage / flood / bushfire overlays), rental yields, and address-level detail.
          • Crime is council-level, not suburb-level — a planned refinement.
