    How to use Liveable Melbourne

    This tool helps you compare Melbourne suburbs across two main goals:

    • Live or rent — where is it safer and more stable for you or your family?
    • Invest or develop — where is there genuine potential for growth or redevelopment?

    Switch views using the mode buttons and presets. Everything updates instantly.

    Quick tips

    • Click any suburb on the map or in the list to see its full scorecard.
    • Use the slider to blend Liveability and Development Potential.
    • Try the one-click presets (Family First, Balanced Investor, Value-Add/Developer).
    • Toggle the electricity network to see transmission lines and substations.

    Colouring the map

    The map colour changes with what you select:

    • Live mode — emphasises safety, socio-economic advantage and family-friendly areas (green tones).
    • Balanced mode — a 50/50 mix of liveability and development potential.
    • Invest / Develop mode — highlights redevelopment headroom, price growth and infrastructure support (blue-gold tones).

    Key controls

    • Slider: how much weight you give Liveability vs Development.
    • Colour by: what the map colours represent (Overall, Safety, Growth, Grid…).
    • Hide weaker areas: focus only on stronger suburbs.
    • Electricity overlay: show major transmission lines and substations.

    What the scores mean (plain English)

    Liveability Score

    Higher = better for living or renting. Combines:

    • Lower rates of crimes against the person
    • Higher socio-economic advantage (SEIFA)
    • More stable, owner-occupied housing
    • Higher share of families with children (in Family mode)
    Development Potential Score

    Higher = more signals the area may have room to grow or be redeveloped. Combines:

    • Higher proportion of detached houses (redevelopment headroom)
    • Recent price growth momentum
    • Closer proximity to major electricity transmission infrastructure
    • Higher rental turnover (investor activity)

    Still preliminary — it does not yet include full planning zoning or overlays.

    Overall Score

    A blend of the two scores above. You control the balance with the slider or presets.

    Chip & term glossary

    Very safe / Safe — Low rates of personal crime
    Growth corridor — Strong recent price growth + redevelopment headroom
    Grid-ready — Close to major transmission lines and multiple substations
    Redevelopment headroom — High share of detached houses on larger blocks
    Affordable upside — Relatively lower prices with growth momentum
    Premium market — High prices, typically established or blue-chip areas
    Tightly held — High owner-occupier rates, lower rental turnover
    Family-friendly — Higher share of families with children + strong safety signals

    Where the data comes from

    Electricity Infrastructure

    Geoscience Australia — National Electricity Transmission Lines and Substations (2024). Proximity to transmission lines and density of substations within 10 km are used as practical signals for development feasibility. Detailed substation capacity data is generally not publicly available.

    AEMO context is referenced for the broader electricity network; geometry comes from Geoscience Australia.

    Property Prices & Growth

    Victorian Valuer-General / Land.Vic — Median house and unit prices by suburb (2014–2024). 12-month change and 3-year CAGR are calculated from this data.

    Liveability inputs
    • Crime Statistics Agency Victoria (CSA) — Recorded offences by LGA, split into crimes against the person vs property crime (year ending March 2026).
    • Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) — SEIFA 2021 (IRSAD & IEO), Census 2021 housing (G37) and age demographics (G01).
    Geography

    ABS ASGS Edition 3 (2021) — Statistical Area Level 2 (SA2) boundaries for Greater Melbourne.

    What’s still preliminary / coming next
    Development Potential currently uses housing form, price growth and electricity proximity. It does not yet include full planning controls (zoning, overlays, height limits) from VICPLAN / Vicmap Planning. Those layers are planned next.

    All data is public and government-sourced. Scores are percentile-ranked within Greater Melbourne only.
